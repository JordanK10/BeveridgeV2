MULTI-BASIN OLS FITTING ALGORITHM
==================================
src/calibration/fit.py

Overview
--------
The goal is to fit three model parameters — K, c_exponent (α_C), and idio_std
— to empirical unemployment and vacancy time series by minimizing a
sum-of-squared-residuals (OLS) objective. The remaining parameters are fixed:

    s                = 0.01   (separation rate)
    zero_fraction    = 0.50   (fraction of GDP-insensitive firms)
    firing_threshold = 0.00   (instantaneous firing)
    c_max            = 1.00   (power-law scale; shape controlled by c_exponent)

Because the simulation is non-differentiable and the loss surface may be
multimodal, the algorithm is designed to find several distinct local optima
rather than a single global minimum.

The pipeline has three stages.


Stage 1: Latin Hypercube Scan
------------------------------
The 3D parameter space is explored coarsely using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS).
LHS divides each parameter dimension into n equal intervals and places exactly one
sample per interval, guaranteeing uniform marginal coverage. For n=500 points this
gives far better coverage of the 3D space than a grid (a 16^3 grid would require
4096 evaluations for equivalent resolution, but LHS also avoids clustering).

Free parameter bounds:
    K             : [2.00, 7.00]
    c_exponent    : [0.25, 1.00]
    idio_std      : [0.001, 0.06]

At each sample point the simulation is run once and eight losses are computed
simultaneously:

    mse_u          = mean( w * (u_sim - u_emp)^2 )
    mse_v          = mean( w * (v_sim - v_emp)^2 )
    mse_combined   = mean( w * (a*(u_sim - u_emp) + b*(v_sim - v_emp))^2 )
    mse_du         = mean( w' * (Δu_sim - Δu_emp)^2 )
    mse_dv         = mean( w' * (Δv_sim - Δv_emp)^2 )
    mse_d_combined = mean( w' * (a*(Δu_sim - Δu_emp) + b*(Δv_sim - Δv_emp))^2 )
    mse_bc         = mean( w  * ((u_sim-u_emp)^2 + (v_sim-v_emp)^2) )
    mse_flow       = mean( w' * ((Δu_sim-Δu_emp)^2 + (Δv_sim-Δv_emp)^2) )

where a and b are user-specified weights (default a=b=0.5), Δ denotes the
first difference (month-to-month change), w is an optional per-period sample
weight vector, and w' is the corresponding weight for difference periods
(average of adjacent w values). The first-difference objectives penalise models
that produce a flat trajectory near the empirical mean without capturing
recession/recovery dynamics. Running all eight losses from a single simulation
pass means the scan cost is the same regardless of how many objectives are
optimized.

The Beveridge-space objectives (mse_bc, mse_flow) treat (u,v) as a joint 2D
point rather than two independent scalars. The critical difference from
mse_combined and mse_d_combined is that residuals cannot cancel across
dimensions: a model that overshoots u while undershooting v by the same amount
scores zero on mse_combined (the errors cancel in the dot product) but is
correctly penalised by mse_bc. mse_bc targets the static geometry of the
Beveridge curve; mse_flow targets the direction, magnitude, and persistence of
movement through Beveridge space at each time step.

Sample weights can be used to down-weight specific episodes. In practice, the
acute COVID shock (Apr–Aug 2020) is set to weight 0.1 because the labour-market
spike in those months is partly a policy/lockdown effect the matching model is
not designed to capture; the March 2020 GDP drop is the signal the model should
respond to.

The scan is parallelised across 8 workers.


Stage 2: Basin Identification
------------------------------
From the scan results, N distinct low-loss parameter neighbourhoods (basins) are
identified for each of the six objectives independently using a greedy
max-separation algorithm:

    1. Sort all scan evaluations by the chosen objective (ascending).
    2. Restrict to the top fraction (default: lowest 20%) as candidates.
    3. Seed with the globally best candidate.
    4. Iteratively select the candidate whose minimum normalised distance to any
       already-selected seed is largest — i.e. always pick the point that is most
       different from what has already been selected.
    5. Reject candidates whose minimum distance falls below a separation threshold
       (default: 0.15, meaning 15% of the normalised parameter range).
    6. Repeat until N basins are found.
    7. Interior-basin check: if every selected seed has at least one parameter
       within 5% of a bound (i.e. all seeds are "boundary points"), force-inject
       the best-loss candidate that is interior (all parameters > 5% from every
       bound). This guarantees that Nelder-Mead always explores at least one
       non-boundary region, even when the global optimum appears to lie at a boundary.

Distances are computed in normalised parameter space (each dimension scaled to
[0,1] by its bounds) so that parameters with different units and scales are
treated comparably.

This procedure guarantees that basin seeds are both low-loss and well-separated
in parameter space, making it unlikely that two seeds represent the same local
optimum. The interior-basin injection additionally ensures that boundary crowding
does not prevent exploration of the parameter interior.


Stage 3: Local Refinement
--------------------------
Each basin seed is refined independently using Nelder-Mead (downhill simplex)
optimisation. Nelder-Mead is chosen because:

    - It is gradient-free and handles non-differentiable objectives.
    - It works well in low-dimensional spaces (3 parameters here).
    - It does not require finite-difference gradient approximations, which would
      be noisy given the stochastic simulation.

Parameters are clipped to their bounds at every function evaluation. The
refinement terminates when the simplex diameter falls below 1e-5 or the function
value change falls below 1e-8, or after 500 iterations.

Each refined basin reports:
    - The optimal parameter vector.
    - The loss under all six objectives (even if only one was optimized).
    - Whether the optimizer converged.
    - The number of iterations taken.
    - seed_interior: whether the LHS seed was interior (all params > 5% from bounds).
    - at_boundary: whether Nelder-Mead converged to a boundary point.


Outputs
-------
Results are saved to output/fit/ (or the specified output_dir):
    lhs_scan.csv              — all LHS evaluations with all eight losses
    basins_mse_u.csv          — refined basins for the u level objective
    basins_mse_v.csv          — refined basins for the v level objective
    basins_mse_combined.csv   — refined basins for the combined level objective
    basins_mse_du.csv         — refined basins for the Δu objective
    basins_mse_dv.csv         — refined basins for the Δv objective
    basins_mse_d_combined.csv — refined basins for the combined Δ objective
    basins_mse_bc.csv         — refined basins for the Beveridge-space distance objective
    basins_mse_flow.csv       — refined basins for the Beveridge-space flow objective

Each basin CSV includes seed_interior and at_boundary columns to make it easy
to identify boundary vs. interior solutions.

The eight sets of basins can be compared to identify parameter regions that are
robust across objectives versus those that only fit one series or one type of
moment (level vs. dynamics vs. Beveridge-space geometry) well.


Interpreting Multiple Basins
-----------------------------
Because the Beveridge curve is an equilibrium relationship between u and v, there
can be multiple parameter combinations that reproduce the empirical trajectory
approximately equally well. For example:

    - High K (fast matching) with high s (fast separation) can produce similar
      steady-state unemployment to low K with low s.
    - Low c_exponent (many sensitive firms) can mimic high c_exponent (few very
      sensitive firms) at the aggregate level when zero_fraction is adjusted.

The multi-basin approach surfaces these degeneracies explicitly. Basins that
appear consistently across the level objectives (mse_u, mse_v) and the
first-difference objectives (mse_du, mse_dv) are more likely to represent
genuinely distinct mechanisms rather than artifacts of the particular loss
function used. A basin that fits levels well but not dynamics (or vice versa)
signals a structural mismatch between the model and the data for that region
of parameter space.


NEW IDEA

\subsection{Fitting Beveridge-Space Dynamics Instead of Marginal Time Series}

A limitation of fitting unemployment and vacancies independently through
pointwise mean-squared error objectives,
\[
u(t), \qquad v(t),
\]
is that the optimization primarily rewards matching long-run averages and
low-frequency trends, rather than correctly reproducing the intertemporal
response dynamics of the labor market. In practice, this can produce simulated
trajectories that remain near the empirical mean while failing to capture the
timing, persistence, hysteresis, and geometric structure of recession and
recovery episodes.

To address this, we instead propose fitting the dynamics directly in
\emph{Beveridge space}. Rather than treating unemployment and vacancies as two
separate marginal time series, the empirical target becomes the trajectory
\[
(u(t),v(t)),
\]
or equivalently the dynamical response rule
\[
(\Delta u_t,\Delta v_t)
=
F(u_t,v_t,G_t;\theta),
\]
where \(G_t\) is the aggregate GDP signal and \(\theta\) denotes the model
parameters.

The objective is then no longer merely to reproduce the level of unemployment
or vacancies at each date, but rather to reproduce the \emph{local flow field}
of the labor market in Beveridge space. A natural loss function is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{flow}}
=
\sum_t
\left\|
\begin{pmatrix}
\Delta u^{\mathrm{sim}}_t \\
\Delta v^{\mathrm{sim}}_t
\end{pmatrix}
-
\begin{pmatrix}
\Delta u^{\mathrm{emp}}_t \\
\Delta v^{\mathrm{emp}}_t
\end{pmatrix}
\right\|^2,
\]
which directly penalizes the model whenever it predicts the wrong direction,
magnitude, or persistence of movement through Beveridge space.

More generally, the fitting objective can combine level, geometric, and
dynamical information:
\[
\mathcal{L}
=
\lambda_1
\sum_t
\left(
u^{\mathrm{sim}}_t-u^{\mathrm{emp}}_t
\right)^2
+
\lambda_2
\sum_t
\left(
v^{\mathrm{sim}}_t-v^{\mathrm{emp}}_t
\right)^2
+
\lambda_3
\sum_t
d_{\mathrm{BC}}
\!\left(
(u^{\mathrm{sim}}_t,v^{\mathrm{sim}}_t),
(u^{\mathrm{emp}}_t,v^{\mathrm{emp}}_t)
\right)^2
+
\lambda_4
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{flow}},
\]
where \(d_{\mathrm{BC}}\) denotes a geometric distance in Beveridge space and
\(\lambda_i\) are weighting parameters.

This approach shifts the fitting target away from static trajectory matching
and toward reproducing the underlying labor-market adjustment dynamics.
Importantly, it allows the calibration procedure to distinguish between models
that merely reproduce average unemployment levels and models that correctly
capture recession amplification, recovery persistence, Beveridge-curve
rotation, and hysteresis dynamics.
