ANTOINE'S APPROACH
──────────────────
Data:
  - Unemployment + vacancy: us_bev_curve.csv (BLS, monthly, 1951–present)
  - GDP: GDP_us_long.csv (FRED quarterly, 1947–2025)

GDP pipeline:
  1. HP-filter quarterly GDP (λ=1600) to extract business cycle component
  2. Rolling z-score normalization (120-quarter = 30-year window)
  3. Linear interpolation of quarterly cycle to monthly
  4. Take time derivative dG/dt; feed this (not G itself) into model

Fitting approach:
  - HAND-TUNED. No formal optimization.
  - Parameters chosen by visual inspection of Beveridge curve shape and
    u/v time series alignment with empirical data.
  - Fixed parameters: s=0.0084, K=0.5 or 2.0, C_u=2, v0=0.0089, C_v=2
  - Validation: qualitative match of Beveridge curve loop shape and
    reproduction of the positive skewness of Δu relative to Δv.
  - No loss function, no residual computation, no confidence intervals.

OUR APPROACH (BeveridgeV2)
──────────────────────────
Data:
  - Unemployment: BLS CPS API (LNS14000000, monthly)
  - Vacancies: JOLTS Job Openings Rate (JTS000000000000000JOR, local Excel)
  - GDP: FRED API (GDPC1, quarterly)

GDP pipeline:
  1. Linear interpolation of quarterly GDP to monthly (time method)
  2. 3-month log-difference: G(t) = log(GDP_t) - log(GDP_{t-3})
  3. Normalize to mean 0, std 0.25
  (No HP filter; derivative not taken — G enters as level, not rate of change)

Fitting approach:
  - scipy.optimize.minimize (L-BFGS-B) or differential_evolution
  - Loss function: weighted sum of MSE(u_sim, u_obs) + MSE(v_sim, v_obs)
                   + correlation difference + Beveridge shape residual
  - Free parameters: s, c_max, c_exponent, zero_fraction
  - Fixed: K=3.5, n_firms=250, dt=1.0
  - 1000-step burn-in at initial GDP level before simulating
  - Validation: trajectory plots and diagnostic_summary.txt

Findings:
  1. UNEMPLOYMENT VARIANCE: All model variants drastically underestimate the
     volatility of u. Empirical std(u)=0.018; our baseline gives 0.005. This
     means the model is not transmitting enough of the GDP signal into u swings.
     Root cause: c_max=0.08 is too small. With c_max=0.4 (new upper bound),
     calibration should be able to increase GDP-to-unemployment pass-through.

  2. VACANCY LEVEL: Our baseline v_mean=0.055 is 1.8× the empirical 0.031.
     Antoine's K=0.5 run gives v=0.21 (7× too high). The vacancy level is
     determined at steady state by s/(s + K*u). With s=0.01, K=3.5, u=0.06:
     v_ss ≈ 0.01/(0.01 + 3.5*0.06) = 0.044. To hit v=0.031 with s=0.01,
     K would need to be ~5.0 or s would need to drop to ~0.006.

  3. VACANCY SKEWNESS: All models produce large negative skew(Δv) while
     empirical skew(Δv) is near zero (+0.10). This is a systematic model
     failure likely arising from vacancy posting mechanics: vacancies collapse
     suddenly when matching absorbs them, creating asymmetric drops.

  4. u-v CORRELATION: Our baseline achieves −0.58, closer to −0.75 target
     than Antoine's K=0.5 (−0.30) or K=2.0 (−0.44). This is our strongest
     result so far.

  5. Δu SKEWNESS: All models get the sign right (positive), meaning they
     reproduce the empirical asymmetry of unemployment rises vs falls.
     Our baseline (+0.83) is somewhat higher than empirical (+0.43).

  6. ANTOINE'S PARAMETERS IN OUR MODEL: Running s=0.0084, K=0.5 through
     our agent-based simulator produces vacancy rates of ~21% — completely
     unrealistic. This is because our matching function m=K*u (linear in u)
     gives much weaker vacancy absorption than Antoine's bilinear m=K*u*v,
     so low K fails to clear vacancies. K=2.0 is better (v~7.5%) but still
     2.4× too high. This confirms that the two models are not directly
     parameter-comparable.

4. RECOMMENDED NEXT STEPS (ordered by expected impact)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1 — Run the calibration optimizer [HIGH PRIORITY]
  The baseline parameters (c_max=0.08, s=0.01) are known to be off. The
  optimizer now has access to c_max ∈ [0.05, 0.40] and should be able to
  find parameters that dramatically improve u variance and corr(u,v).
  Command: python -m src.calibration.main
  Expected improvement: std(u) should reach 0.010–0.015, corr(u,v) → −0.70+

STEP 2 — Add skew(Δv) to the loss function [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  Currently the loss function targets MSE(u), MSE(v), and corr(u,v). The
  negative skew(Δv) failure will not be penalized. Adding a term for
  |skew(Δu) - target_skew_u| + |skew(Δv) - target_skew_v| would push
  calibration toward fixing the asymmetry.

STEP 3 — Fix the vacancy steady-state level [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  The equilibrium vacancy rate is structurally too high. Options:
    (a) Add K to the calibrated parameters (currently fixed at 3.5).
        Optimal K is likely 4.5–6.0 to bring v_ss to ~0.031.
    (b) Allow s to calibrate below 0.005 (current lower bound).
    (c) Switch matching to bilinear m = K*u*v (Antoine's formulation),
        which naturally clears vacancies faster without requiring high K.

STEP 4 — Match Antoine's GDP signal construction [LOW–MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  Our quarterly log-diff G(t) captures business cycles but lacks Antoine's
  smoothing via HP filter + rolling z-score. The HP filter emphasizes
  persistent cycles over high-frequency noise. This may improve the temporal
  structure of u and v responses. However, the HP filter has end-point bias
  (tested and rejected earlier); could apply it only to the interior of the
  sample with padding.

STEP 5 — Expand the calibration window [LOW PRIORITY]
  Antoine's us_bev_curve.csv has data from 1951 onward. Our JOLTS data begins
  in 2001. Extending back via the BLS Job Openings proxy (pre-JOLTS) would
  give ~50 years of data, improving identification of s and the C distribution.

STEP 6 — Add Δu and Δv skewness to the diagnostic plots [HOUSEKEEPING]
  The overlay_comparison.pdf should show histograms of Δu and Δv for both
  empirical and simulated series, making the skewness gap immediately visible.

5. SUMMARY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Our pipeline has several advantages over Antoine's: formal optimization rather
than hand-tuning, live data fetching from BLS/JOLTS/FRED, and the ability to
test heterogeneous firm C distributions. However, we currently underperform
Antoine's qualitative results on two key dimensions: unemployment variance
(too damped) and vacancy skewness (wrong sign). Both are tractable with
calibration (Step 1) and a modified loss function (Step 2). The biggest
structural question is whether to adopt the bilinear matching function — this
would make our model more directly comparable to Antoine's and is likely
necessary to achieve a correct steady-state vacancy rate without requiring
an unrealistically high K.
